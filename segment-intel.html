Watch-Fors
Owner not present: Many ethnic restaurants are family-run. If the owner isn't there, don't leave a message — come back when they are. The decision-maker must be in the room.
Saturday service: Weekends are typically full for this segment. Avoid calling Fri–Sun. Mon–Wed AM is ideal.
Language: If owner is more comfortable in their native language — find a common ground opener. Food and community resonates universally.
Family consultation: Decision often needs family sign-off. Ask: "Is there anyone else who would want to see this before you decide?"
Opening Angle — Ethnic Restaurants
Opening (Discovery first, product second): "Hi, I'm [Name] from Too Good To Go — do you have 3 minutes? I was looking at your menu and I noticed you do a fantastic weekday lunch [or: weekday service]. I wanted to ask — how does your Monday-Wednesday lunch period compare to weekends? Do you get a lot of covers in that mid-week window?" [Let them answer. If they confirm it's quiet:] "That's really common for great restaurants like yours — the weekday lunch gap is one of the biggest sources of food waste we see. Across [Tooting / Whitechapel / this area], we're helping [X] restaurants in your area recover that revenue. Can I show you what it looks like in numbers for a restaurant like yours?" [Walk through the maths: bag price × bags per day × trading days = monthly revenue that was £0 without TGTG] Mission angle (if owner is receptive): "The thing I love about restaurants like yours is that you already have a culture of not wasting food. TGTG just puts that on a platform where customers actively look for it. Your food, your price — we just fill the gap."
Objection Responses — Ethnic Restaurants
"We don't have leftover food — we cook to order." ▼
"That's exactly the point — TGTG isn't about leftovers. It's about a reserved bag of fresh food you'd normally cook for a time window you know is quiet. You control exactly what goes in, you choose the window, and you set the price. It's not different stock — it's the same quality, just a smarter use of your prep."
"I don't want to discount my food." ▼
"You're not discounting — you're recovering revenue you'd otherwise lose. A quiet Monday lunch where you sell 6 TGTG bags at £4.50 is £27 you didn't have before. The bag isn't discounted in your mind — it's a different channel, like a delivery window that you control. And the customer is choosing TGTG specifically because they value the surprise element, not because they expect a restaurant-menu price."
"We're already busy enough — I don't need more customers." ▼
"Perfect — let's target only your genuinely quiet windows. You set the days and times. If Monday 11am-1pm is always slow, you offer 6 bags in that window only. You're not adding pressure to busy periods — you're converting dead time into revenue."
"What restaurants in this area use it?" ▼
Share 2–3 local examples from your current book (same postcode if possible). "Actually, [Venue Name] down the road has been on since [month] — they're doing [X] bags a week. Would it help if I put you in touch with them directly?" Direct owner testimonials are 3× more convincing than data.

Why This Segment Wins
Highest win rate of all segments (74%): Bakeries are TGTG's natural fit. The product (end-of-day bread, pastries) is perfectly suited to a surprise bag model. Owners get it immediately.
Quick close: Average 6–8 days to close (fastest). High owner familiarity with TGTG concept.

Watch-Fors
Lower deal value: £340/mo is the lowest of strategic segments. Volume play required to make this work for revenue targets.
Higher churn: 12% annual. Often driven by seasonal closures, ownership changes, or feeling the economics aren't worth the admin.
When to Prioritise
Great for volume targets and win rate numbers. When you need Q-end pipeline, bakeries close fast.
Not your primary strategic focus — ethnic food and brunch have better unit economics. Use bakeries to hit volume, not to build a high-value book.

Characteristics
Mid-tier economics: £420/mo is solid but churn at 16% hurts LTV. Net of churn, a pub account is worth less than it appears.
Management layer: Many pubs are chain-managed. Decision-making can be above the venue manager. Identify who signs commercial decisions first.
Brand ambivalence: Some publicans see TGTG as a "food" platform not relevant to them. Need to establish food service angle clearly.
Best Targets
Gastropubs: Strong food offering, already food-brand-conscious. TGTG aligns with their positioning.
Sunday roast specialists: Leftover roast prep is high-value bag content. Mon-Tue offer window (using Sunday prep surplus).
Independent freehouses: Not chain-managed. Decision maker is in the building.
When to Deprioritise
Avoid wet-led pubs (majority of revenue is drink, minimal food). They won't have enough food prep to create meaningful bags.
Avoid chain managed houses (Stonegate, Mitchells & Butlers) — requires national agreement, not rep relationship. Flag to Country Director if a chain approaches you.

Strategic Role: Volume Lever
Highest volume opportunity: 2,140 venues, 71% win rate, quick 8–12 day close cycle. When you need to hit quantity targets, this is your segment.
Retention challenge: 14% annual churn — the highest of any strategic segment. Owner fatigue, competition from other platforms, low margin consciousness. Must build in quarterly check-in cadence.
Good for new reps: Shorter sales cycle = faster learning. Perfect for ramping phone reps in their first 30 days.
Unit Economics Warning
£380/mo × 12% commission = £45.60/mo. Net of acquisition cost (~£80), payback period is 1.8 months. If they churn at 14%, avg tenure is 8.5 months. Net LTV: £387. Low but positive.
Compare: Ethnic food at £680/mo × 12% = £81.60/mo, 8% churn, 12.5 month avg tenure. Net LTV: £1,020. 2.6× QSR. Focus accordingly.
When to Use This Segment
End-of-quarter volume push. New rep ramp (fast close cycle). Postcode whitespace where ethnic/brunch penetration is already high and QSR remains untouched.
Not your primary strategic play. Use it to supplement, not lead.
