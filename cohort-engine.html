The Director's Lens
What Ruby is actually evaluating
Ruby is a Country Director. She is accountable for UK & Ireland P&L. She needs the Head of Indies to own the engine room — acquisition pipeline, account health, team capacity — so she is never caught off-guard. She does not want to manage this function. She wants to trust the person running it.

The challenge she's described internally: early-stage salespeople who need structure, a culture of resilience that doesn't exist yet, hero-rep dependency that creates fragility, and Team Leads who may not be ready to lead. She needs someone who has solved this before and can do it again — fast.
She will test: People decisions
"What do you do when a rep has three bad weeks in a row?" She wants to know you move fast, are specific, and don't wait for HR to tell you what to do.
She will test: Commercial acumen
Can you talk about marketplace liquidity? Can you see what the data misses? Can you connect team performance to platform health?
She will test: Forecasting confidence
"How do you forecast with early-stage reps who don't know their own pipeline?" She needs a head who produces clean, credible numbers — not rep optimism.
She will test: Culture instinct
She has a mixed team — experienced and very early-stage. She needs someone who can hold both without treating them the same.
Your Answers to Her Likely Questions
"How do you build a coaching culture with early-stage reps?" ▾
"Early-stage salespeople don't fail because they lack ability — they fail because they lack a structure that gives them feedback fast enough. What I build is a daily feedback loop: staggered standups so I can observe each team, call listening as a daily habit not an event, and AI call analysis that means every rep gets scored on every call — not just the ones I hear. The rep who struggles on Monday gets specific coaching by Tuesday standup and a chance to apply it before Thursday. The rep who's having three bad days doesn't spiral — because we catch it at day one and diagnose whether it's skill, confidence, or something personal. I've trained people who didn't know what a sales call was into consistent performers. It takes structure and patience and it takes you believing the person can do it before they believe it themselves."
"How do you prevent hero-rep dependency?" ▾
"The danger isn't the star rep — it's the system that only works when they're there. What I do from week one is make the top performers' methods explicit and transferable. When Marco has a great call, I play 90 seconds of it at Friday standup with the specific technique named. I ask Marco to co-coach Priya on Thursdays. I document the 'why it works' — not just what they said, but the underlying principle. Now the playbook isn't Marco's instinct — it's everyone's framework. And when Marco gets promoted, which he will, the team doesn't drop — because they've been absorbing his method for six months."
"How do you manage Team Leads who aren't ready to lead?" ▾
"Team Leads who've been promoted from the rep floor often manage the way they were managed — which might mean they're too soft, or too focused on their own deals, or they don't know what a 1:1 is supposed to do. I solve this with a structured TL development programme: weekly 1:1 with me specifically on their coaching quality, a shared call review process so they're listening to their reps' calls every day, and a monthly 'manager coaching session' where we role-play difficult conversations before they have them. I also track their team's score improvement — because a good Team Lead's impact shows up in their team's trajectory, not their own numbers."
"How will you keep me informed without me having to ask?" ▾
"You'll have a Friday brief from me every week — pipeline health, team performance, any at-risk accounts or at-risk people, and the 1–2 decisions that need you. It takes you 5 minutes to read. I flag things early — not when they're already a crisis. If I have a difficult people situation, you hear about it from me before you hear about it from anyone else. The commitment I make is: you are never surprised by something I knew about."

Resilience & Motivation
When they're having
several bad days in a row.
Phone sales is one of the most psychologically demanding roles in commercial work. Rejection is the baseline, not the exception. Your job is to build a team that bounces — not one that has to be rescued every time the pipeline goes cold. Here's the practical system.
Early Warning Signals
Dial count drops 30%+ in 3 consecutive days
This is almost never about effort. It's about confidence or something personal. Don't let it reach day 4. A 5-minute check-in call ("I noticed you've been quieter this week — what's going on?") is the intervention.
Rep goes silent in the group Slack
Sales reps who are struggling often withdraw socially before they withdraw commercially. Monitor team Slack participation as an early indicator — not as surveillance, but as care.
Call quality drops 1+ point across consecutive days
Quality decline before volume decline. The rep is still dialling but the energy isn't there. Catch the quality drop before the volume drops.
Rep starts blaming the product, the leads, the market
Attribution shift — they've stopped believing in their own agency. This needs a direct but non-confrontational conversation: "What would need to be different for you to feel like you could win today?"
The Practical Response Toolkit
Day 1 of a bad run
Normalise it explicitly. "Today was hard. That happens. Your job tomorrow is to make one good call — not twenty. Let's listen to your best call from last month to remind you what you sound like when you're in flow."
Day 2–3 of a bad run
Reset the environment. Change the seat, change the time block, change the segment they're calling, change who they're sitting next to. Sometimes the rut is physical and geographical. A different chair on a different floor genuinely breaks the pattern for some people.
Day 4+ — the spiral risk
Remove the target temporarily. "I'm not tracking your dial count today. I want you to make one call that you care about and tell me what happened." Paradoxically, removing the pressure for one session often produces the breakthrough call that restores confidence. Then build back up.
When they're ready to quit
First: listen without fixing. Let them fully articulate what's wrong before you say anything. Often the articulation itself releases the pressure. Then: "What was the best week you've had here? What was different?" Return them to their own evidence that they can do this.
Structural Resilience Builders
Daily wins board (physical, in office)
Not a leaderboard (which demoralises the bottom). A wins board — where anyone can post any win, however small. First call of the day. Best opening question. A callback they got after 5 attempts. Visibility of progress is the fuel.
Friday call reel (coaching culture)
Play 2 clips of excellent calls at Friday standup. Name the rep, name the specific technique. The rep whose call is played becomes the standard — not the exception. This makes coaching aspirational, not clinical.
Monthly individual 1:1 (separate from performance)
One 1:1 per month with no pipeline agenda. Just: "How are you? What's going well outside of work? What do you want to be doing in 12 months?" People who feel seen stay longer. People who feel like a number leave at the first opportunity.
Culture across the mixed team (mature + young reps)
Younger reps need more affirmation and faster feedback loops. More experienced reps need autonomy and respect for what they already know — don't train them like they've never sold. Segment your motivation strategy the same way you segment your market.
What Not To Do
Don't increase the pressure when someone is struggling
A rep having three bad days who gets a "you need to hit your numbers" message from their manager has three worse days. The pressure that creates results when someone is confident is the pressure that creates exits when someone is fragile.
Don't ignore it either
The opposite error is saying nothing because you don't want to "make it a bigger deal." A rep who has three bad days and gets no acknowledgement from their manager learns that their manager isn't paying attention. Both interpretations are damaging.
Don't make it public
Never name a struggling rep in front of the team. Recognition is public. Coaching conversations are private. The distinction is the foundation of psychological safety — the condition under which people try things they're not sure about yet.
Don't compare to other reps
"Sarah has been doing X — why aren't you?" is the single most demoralising sentence a manager can say. Compare to their own previous best — never to a peer.
Dependency Management
No single point
of failure.
Hero rep dependency is the structural risk of high-performing sales teams. When Marco closes 40% of all deals, you don't have a team — you have Marco and some people who watch him. The promotion play compounds this: when Marco gets promoted (which he should), velocity drops unless you've already transferred his method to the team. Here's how to prevent both.
Signals You Have Hero Dependency
One rep closes >35% of team's monthly revenue
Team win rate drops >10pts when that rep is absent
Newer reps defer to the hero rep instead of calling the manager
The hero rep's technique is described as "natural" — not documented
No one else on the team can handle the hero's most complex deals
The Fix: Method Extraction
Name the technique: When Marco has a great call, isolate the specific thing he did. "The moment he referenced Google Popular Times before asking about quiet periods — that's the technique. Let's call it the Data-First Open."
Document it explicitly: Add it to the Gold Standard. Named after the rep who invented it. Now it's institutional knowledge.
Teach it through the hero: Marco runs the session. He owns the narrative. He's not losing his edge — he's building the team's edge from his.
Measure transfer: Track whether Priya and James are using the "Data-First Open" 4 weeks later. If yes, the method transferred. If no, retrain.
Promotion Without Velocity Loss
Promote into coaching, not out of selling (initially): Marco spends 60% in management mode, 40% on his own deals for 60 days. The team has a bridge period, not a cliff edge.
Promote Sarah before Marco leaves: The TL seat doesn't sit empty. Sarah is ready. She's coached for it. The succession is planned, not reactive.
Transfer his best accounts to the team: Marco's top 3 accounts go to the best-suited rep (Priya for ethnic, Sarah for brunch) with Marco's personal introduction. The relationship transfers, not just the record.
The metric you're watching: Team win rate 4 weeks post-promotion. If it stays within 5pts, the transfer was clean. If it drops more, you have a method dependency — not just a Marco dependency.
For Team Leads: The Same Risk
If your Team Lead is the strongest coach: The team can't develop without them — that's a dependency. TL must teach their reps to self-coach using the AI call analysis tool.
If your TL is your best forecaster: What happens when they're on holiday? Build the forecast ritual into the team, not just the TL's brain.
If your TL is the only one with deep partner relationships: Introduce the team to top accounts. Relationship is the TL's — but familiarity is the team's. Partners should know at least two names at TGTG.

AI in the Sales Process
What AI can do.
What only you can do.
AI gives back time and surfaces patterns faster than any manager can at scale. But the things that close deals — reading the hesitation in an owner's voice, knowing when to stay quiet, sensing that the family isn't behind the decision — those remain entirely human. Here's the precise boundary.
🔍
Pre-Call Intelligence Generation
Before any call, a rep types a venue name and AI surfaces: Google Popular Times data (their actual quiet periods), review sentiment (what customers say about weekday service), Instagram activity patterns (which days they post vs go dark — often mirrors covers), competitor TGTG listings nearby, and a suggested opening question specific to this venue type. What used to take 15 minutes of manual research takes 45 seconds. The rep arrives at the call knowing things the owner doesn't know you know.
Time saved: 15–20 min/call · Redirected to: more dials, better preparation
📊
Post-Call Transcript Scoring (AI Call Analysis)
Every call transcript is scored against the 7 criteria framework. AI identifies: whether discovery questions were asked, whether the economics were walked through, whether an offer window was agreed, and what the rep said that landed vs what fell flat. A rep who previously waited 2 days for coaching feedback now has it within 60 minutes. A manager who could listen to 5 calls a day can now surface insights from 50. The coach doesn't replace the call analysis — they use it to direct their coaching energy.
Adoption required: All reps submit call recordings · Manager reviews AI flags daily
🔄
Churn Prediction (Partner Side)
AI monitors partner booking patterns and flags accounts where weekly bag volume has dropped 30%+, weekly active days have fallen, or the offer window hasn't been updated in 3+ weeks. These accounts go into the Partner Revival queue. The AM team doesn't have to manually review 156 accounts — they work from a prioritised list of the 8–12 who need contact this week. This shifts the AM team from reactive to genuinely proactive.
Immediate win for adoption: Will Carter's churn issue would have been caught 3 weeks earlier
📈
Pipeline Forecast Weighting
Rep pipeline data + AI stage-weighting + stale-deal penalty = the number you give Ruby. AI applies historical win rates by segment and deal stage to produce a weighted forecast. A rep who says "£80K" and has 6 deals all marked Stage 2 gets a 48% probability weighting applied automatically. You present weighted reality, not rep optimism. Ruby gets a forecast she can rely on.
Outcome: Director trust · No surprises · Clean management cadence
🧠
Segment & Territory Intelligence
AI surfaces the highest-density untapped territories for each segment, time-of-day call success rates (ethnic restaurants: Monday AM is 43% better conversion than Friday PM), and competitor TGTG listing gaps in specific areas. This turns territory planning from intuition to evidence. Reps stop guessing which streets to prioritise — they have a ranked list before Monday morning.
Applied in: Segment Intelligence tool · Territory Planner · Pre-call brief
👁️
What AI Cannot See — Your Job
AI cannot hear the micro-hesitation in an owner's voice that tells you they want to say yes but haven't talked to their partner yet. It cannot see that the chef is standing behind the owner and this decision will need his buy-in too. It cannot feel that the owner is proud and needs to be asked, not sold. It cannot sense that the rep is scared of this particular call and needs a 2-minute confidence reset before they dial. The data is the map. You are the compass.
Human intelligence: irreplaceable · AI intelligence: essential · Both: unstoppable

Director Communication Principles
Flag early, not when it's already a crisis. If Will Carter's churn rate concerned you this week, Ruby hears about it this week — not when three more accounts have left. Early flagging is a sign of control, not weakness.
Tell her what you don't know. "I don't have enough data on the non-food segment yet — I'll have a recommendation in 3 weeks" builds more trust than a premature recommendation that later has to be walked back.
Bring decisions, not situations. "Here's a problem" puts the cognitive load on Ruby. "Here's a problem, here are 3 options, here's my recommendation, here's what I need from you" respects her time and demonstrates your authority.
The Friday brief is non-negotiable. It happens even in the weeks where everything is fine — especially then. The pattern of reliable communication is what builds the trust that gives you autonomy. Miss it twice and she starts asking for updates herself. That is the beginning of micromanagement.
