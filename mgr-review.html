Q2How would you run a 26-person phone sales team differently from what's already there?›
Your answer
I'd start by listening, not changing. You've said this is a motivated, high-performing team — the worst thing I could do is walk in with a playbook from somewhere else and start rewriting processes that are already working. Week one is observation. I sit with each Team Lead, listen to calls, read the Salesforce data, understand where the conversion drop-offs are, and understand the team's own sense of what's blocking them.

Then I'd focus on two things. First, the Team Leads. A team of 26 is really a team of 3. If I develop the three Team Leads well, the 23 reps will follow. I'd run weekly 1:1s with each TL focused on their team's conversion metrics, their coaching habits — not just results. Are they listening to calls? Are they running structured debriefs? Second, the funnel. In phone-based acquisition the problem is almost always at one stage — either not enough dials, connect rate is low, pitch quality is inconsistent, or the close is weak. I'd map every rep's conversion at each stage and the coaching becomes very specific. Not "you need to do better" — "your connect rate is fine, your pitch-to-commitment conversion is 40% below team average, here's what's happening in that part of the call."
Tip: Lead with listening. They want someone who will develop what's there, not replace it. Show you understand the 3 TL leverage point.

Q4How do you develop Team Leads, not just manage them?›
Your answer
The distinction is important and most managers get it wrong. Managing a Team Lead means reviewing their team's numbers. Developing a Team Lead means building their ability to coach, to diagnose problems, and to have difficult conversations.

I do three things with Team Leads that I don't do with reps. First, I run their 1:1 differently. We don't spend the majority of time on their team's pipeline — we spend it on their leadership decisions. What did they coach this week? What was the outcome? What would they do differently? I want them developing a coaching habit, not just a reporting habit.

Second, I involve them in the strategic layer. At OYO, my Team Leads sat in on the commercial planning sessions. They understood the market-level targets, not just their team target. That context changes how they lead — they can explain the why, not just the what.

Third, I give them real ownership with real accountability. Their team's conversion rate is theirs to own. I'll support, I'll coach, I'll identify the problem with them — but I won't solve it for them. The moment a manager solves problems for their Team Lead, the TL stops growing.
Q5How do you balance new partner acquisition with retaining and growing existing ones?›
Your answer
They're not competing priorities — they're complementary if you structure them correctly. The mistake is letting the acquisition team and the account team operate in silos with different KPIs and no shared accountability.

At Booking.com I managed this dual motion across 6 markets simultaneously. The acquisition team brought partners in. The account team drove their first transaction, their first repeat, their habit of updating availability. But the acquisition team's KPI wasn't just "signed" — it was "signed and active within 14 days." That one change made acquisition reps care about partner quality, not just volume. They stopped signing deals that weren't going to work.

For TGTG specifically, I'd think about this differently to most food platforms. A partner that uploads surplus consistently for 12 weeks is worth 10x a partner that signs and then goes quiet. So the account team's job isn't just retention — it's habit formation. Getting partners to the point where uploading their surplus on TGTG is as automatic as locking up at night. That takes structured onboarding, a check-in at day 7 and day 30, and a clear escalation when upload frequency drops.
Q6Tell me about a time you localised a global best practice for the UK market.›

Situation: Booking.com was expanding into new verticals — Experiences, Transport, Restaurants — globally. The global team had one model: launch everywhere simultaneously. The problem was that some cities had supply but no demand infrastructure. Some had app penetration but no supply. The approach was generating waste — launches that failed in month two because the fundamentals weren't there.

Task: I was Country Manager for UK & Ireland, but the problem was a global one. I was asked to find a better approach.

Action: I built the City Landscape Framework — a market readiness scoring model. Before any new vertical or city launch, you scored the market across five dimensions: existing partner density, app download penetration, local competitor presence, price sensitivity, and regulatory environment. A city below a threshold score didn't get launched — it got a 90-day preparation plan first. I piloted it in Edinburgh and Dublin before the London rollout.

For TGTG, this maps directly. I'd apply the same thinking to new city launches — what's the minimum partner density before we turn on paid acquisition? What's the app download threshold before we expect organic partner demand?
Why this is the best answer: It's specific, it has a global outcome, and it maps directly to TGTG's "localise global best practices" language.
Q7How do you drive a metrics-based culture without killing team morale?›
Your answer
Metrics kill morale when they're used to punish people without context. They build culture when they're used to diagnose problems and give people a clear picture of where they are.

The shift I made at OYO — and it made a significant difference — was making the metrics transparent and directional, not retrospective and judgemental. Every rep could see their own conversion funnel every morning. Not as a comparison with others, but as a question: where is my drop-off? That turned a metric from a number someone uses to tell you off into a tool you use to understand your own performance.

I also separate lagging from leading indicators. Deals signed is a lagging indicator — by the time you see it, you can't change it this week. Dials and connect rates are leading indicators — you can change them today. Teams that only track deals signed feel out of control. Teams that track dials and conversion feel like they have agency. That agency is what creates a positive metrics culture.
Q8What's your approach to underperformance?›
Your answer
Early, specific, and diagnostic — not punitive. The worst thing you can do with an underperforming rep is wait until it becomes a formal conversation. By then you've wasted their time, the team's time, and the company's money.

My approach has three stages. First, diagnose before you judge. Is this a skill gap, a will gap, or a circumstance? A rep who was performing and has stopped is a different problem to a rep who has never performed. The intervention is completely different.

Second, be specific and early. "Your numbers are low" is not a coaching conversation. "Your connect rate is fine but you're losing 60% of calls at the pitch stage — specifically when the partner raises the time commitment objection" is a coaching conversation. That specificity can only happen if you're listening to calls.

Third, give genuine support before formal process. Formal performance management should feel like a last resort, not a first response. If someone isn't performing after genuine, specific, structured support — then you move to formal process quickly and cleanly. But you earn the right to do that by having done the work first.
Q9How do you coach phone sales specifically — what do you listen for?›
Your answer
Phone sales coaching is almost entirely about the first 30 seconds and the moment the partner raises their first objection. Everything else tends to be fine if those two points work.

In the opening, I listen for three things: did they name the specific problem the partner has (surplus going to waste, end-of-day food thrown away), did they earn the right to keep talking, and did they sound like they were reading a script or having a conversation. The TGTG pitch is actually easier to open than most — the problem is universally understood and the mission gives you a natural hook that most sales calls don't have.

At the first objection, I listen for whether the rep panics and capitulates, holds their frame, or gets defensive. The right response is none of those — it's curious. "That's interesting — can you tell me more about that concern?" Most objections in TGTG acquisition are about time, about brand, or about whether it's worth the effort. All of those are addressable if the rep stays curious and doesn't rush to solve.

I also listen for silence. The best phone salespeople are comfortable with silence. They ask a question and they wait. Most reps fill the silence and answer their own question. That's the coaching moment.
