What Not To Do In This Meeting
Don't ask to be briefed on things you can find yourself
Asking Chelsea to explain the team structure or the product is a signal you haven't done the work. Arrive knowing it. Ask only for what isn't available from the outside.
Don't commit to a number before you've seen the pipeline
If Chelsea asks for a revenue target for month one, the answer is: "I'll give you that number after I've done the pipeline audit — within two weeks of starting. I won't commit to a number I can't stand behind." This is honesty. Committing to a number you invented in the meeting and missing it is far worse.
Don't talk about what you're going to do more than what you've already observed
Every candidate says what they'll do. You've already done things — the pre-work, the segment analysis, the market research, the tool-building. Lead with evidence, not intention.
Don't try to impress her with the plan — let the questions impress her
The quality of your questions is the strongest signal of the quality of your thinking. A Country Director who is asked "what's the one thing I won't find in the data?" knows she's sitting with someone who thinks at the right level.

Pipeline Audit — Every Deal Reviewed, No Exceptions
This is the most commercially important action in the first 30 days. A pipeline full of stale, unqualified, or hero-rep-dependent deals is not a pipeline — it's an optimism register. You are going to make it real.
Deal StageWhat You're Looking ForAction
No next step / last contact >7d Deal exists in CRM with no follow-up booked and no owner contact in over a week. Likely dead. Kill or reactivate in 48h
Offer window discussed but not agreed Rep has had the pitch conversation but left the decision to the owner. Classic soft stall. Rep reactivation call this week
Stage 2+ but no offer window booked Rep has qualified the venue but hasn't closed the window. This is the most common failure point. Coaching focus — close in the room
Single-rep dependency (Marco, TL-owned) Top 20% of pipeline is owned by 1–2 reps. If they leave, so does the revenue. Redistribute + document method
Strong stage, clear next step, booked follow-up Real deal. Right economics. Rep knows it, owner wants it. Just needs to close. Protect and close this month

Run pipeline review with each TL, deal by deal. Not a top-level summary — every single deal in each team's active book. The TL presents; you ask: "What's the next concrete action? When? Who owns it?" Any deal without a clear answer gets killed or given a 48-hour reactivation deadline. You're not being brutal — you're being honest about what's real vs. what's wishful.
Name the deals you're killing publicly to the TL. "This deal has had no contact in 9 days and no next step. We're closing it. What do we learn from it?" The TL sees that you're serious about pipeline hygiene. The message goes down to reps through the TL.
Build the first clean forecast. After the audit: stage-weighted, stale-deal penalised, hero-dependency-adjusted. This number is your baseline. Everything Chelsea sees from here is compared to it.
Coaching Plans — Active for Every Q3 and Q4 Rep by End of Week 2
Q4 reps get immediate intervention: ride-along or joint listening session booked within 48 hours of identifying them. The diagnosis question: skill gap, confidence gap, or effort gap? Each requires a different response. Don't treat them the same.
Q3 reps get a structured plan: specific skill to develop, specific coaching action (roleplay, joint call, AI call review), specific check-in date. No rep should be in Q3 without knowing they're in Q3 and knowing what they're working on.
Q1 and Q2 reps get a development conversation: where are they heading, what's next for them, what do they need from you to get there. The best performers leave when they stop seeing a path. Give them the path now.
Standards — Set Once, Held Consistently
Three non-negotiables communicated at Week 2 standup: (1) Every call logged in CRM within 30 minutes with a next step and a date — no exceptions. (2) Every active deal has a close-by date in the system — if it doesn't, it gets one or gets killed. (3) Call quality below 50 on the scorecard triggers a coaching conversation with the TL that week — not the next review cycle.
Communicate the standard, not the consequence. The first time you set a standard, the message should be about what excellence looks like — not what happens if you miss it. That comes later. The team needs to understand the destination before they understand the cost of not reaching it.

Pressure Tests
What breaks.
How you respond.
These are the scenarios most likely to test the plan. None of them are hypothetical — all of them happen in a role like this. The response is prepared in advance, not improvised when the situation arrives.
A TL resigns in month one
The most common disruption in a new leadership role. Response: Chelsea is briefed same day. Interim structure is clear within 24 hours — you cover the standup yourself until a plan is in place. The next-in-line (the Q1 rep on the TL track) is fast-tracked into an interim role. The team doesn't see a gap — they see a succession that was ready. The lesson: the promotion track exists before you need it, not after.
Month 2 pipeline comes in £80K short of Chelsea's expectation
Don't send a corrected forecast without a diagnosis. Chelsea gets: the actual number, the delta from expectation, the specific reason (2 stalled deals in the ethnic food segment, not yet converted), and the recovery plan (reactivation calls placed, offer window close coaching session run). She never learns about a miss from the number — she learns from you, with context, before the number reaches global.
A rep on PIP complains to Chelsea directly
This happens. Response: Chelsea is already briefed (you told her the PIP was happening in the same week it started). When the rep contacts her, Chelsea has context. She knows the situation, the standard, the timeline, and your reasoning. She can redirect the rep to the process. The rep who goes around you reaches a Country Director who already knows you're handling it correctly.
The ethnic food segment produces only 4 of 12 target closures by week 8
The segment strategy is a hypothesis, not a guarantee. If it's underperforming at week 6: diagnose (pitch problem, territory coverage problem, timing problem, or target quality problem). Adjust the specific element. Don't abandon the segment — adjust the approach. Chelsea gets the adjusted plan, not an apology. "Here's what we learned, here's what we're changing, here's the revised target for Q3."
Global announces a new metric or target mid-90-day plan
Chelsea will manage the global communication. Your job is to understand the new metric quickly and map it to what your team is already doing. Usually new global metrics are reframings of existing work, not entirely new work. Ask Chelsea: "Does this change my team's priorities or just how we report them?" The answer shapes whether you update the plan or just the language.
Three reps have a bad week simultaneously — team morale drops
Don't treat it as a management crisis — treat it as a coaching moment. Friday standup: play the best call from the week (there's always one), name the rep, name the technique specifically. Then: "This is what this team looks like when it's in flow. Next week we build on this." One specific, evidence-based recognition resets a team's week faster than any motivational speech.
The 90-day review goes exceptionally well — Chelsea offers expanded scope
Don't expand before the foundation is solid. The right response: "I'm grateful for the confidence. Here's my recommendation: let me run this function at full pace for one more quarter before we add scope. I want the team you have to be extraordinary before I take on more. That's better for the business and better for me." Turning down expanded scope when the timing is wrong is a sign of maturity, not timidity. Chelsea will trust you more for it.
The Closing Statement
The risk in a phone-first sales team is not low activity.
It is undirected activity.
A team that dials 60 numbers a day but has no segment focus, no call quality feedback, and no offer-window close discipline is generating noise, not revenue. The first 90 days are about direction, not speed. What is the team calling? Why those venues? What exactly do they say? What happens when the owner hesitates? These are the questions this plan answers — before the first call of Day 1 is made.

Chelsea Kerr will see a Head of Indies who arrived knowing what to look for, found it, and started fixing it before being asked. The Friday briefs will arrive without being requested. The pipeline will be trusted because it's been audited. The team will improve because the coaching is specific and persistent.
